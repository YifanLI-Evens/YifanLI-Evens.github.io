One can view $q$ as a generic nonzero complex number. But we are also interested in cases when $q$ is a root of $1$.

This magical identity along with its two special cases, the Euler identities $\mathrm{(I21)}$ and $\mathrm{(I22)}$ are the very beginning of the whole beautiful theory of $q$-series, which indicates that a $q$-series usually have two faces, a face of formal power series, constructed by power expansions, hiding all the information inside the coefficients and usually related with difference equations, and another face of infinite products, which we have seldom seen in classical calculus. With its two faces, $q$-series in fact build a bridge connecting the analytical world and the algebraic world. Further discussion for $q$-series is left for the next section.

q-series

To include $q$-series is not just for curiosity. It appears almost everywhere like difference equations, modular forms, quantum groups and quantum invariants. But we shall start with the Euler identities $\mathrm{(I21)}$ and$(\mathrm{I22})$ we have seen before. They both relate infinite sums with infinite products. In order to make the analytic side of the whole theory meaningful, we usually assume $|q| < 1$, but algebraically, $q$ is still a formal variable.
